About

A Career Built on
Clinical Excellence

Dr Andreas Kyriacou is a Consultant Cardiologist and Cardiac Electrophysiologist at Sheffield Teaching Hospitals NHS Trust — one of the UK's leading tertiary cardiac centres. He holds dual appointments at the Sheffield Arrhythmia Centre and South Yorkshire Heart Centre. He provides both NHS and private cardiac care across the region.

Internationally, Dr Kyriacou practises at the German Medical Institute (GMI) in Limassol, Cyprus, bringing world-class electrophysiology expertise to patients across Cyprus and the wider Eastern Mediterranean region.

His clinical focus encompasses the full spectrum of cardiac arrhythmia management, from complex catheter ablation procedures to advanced cardiac device implantation. He is recognised for his expertise in atrial fibrillation ablation, ventricular tachycardia ablation, and the management of patients with advanced implantable electronic cardiac devices.

Patient Education

Understanding Your
Heart Rhythm

Each animation below shows the characteristic ECG pattern for common conditions treated by Dr Kyriacou. Click any card to learn more.

60–100 bpm

Normal Sinus Rhythm

The heart's natural rhythm, driven by the sinus node — the heart's built-in pacemaker. Each beat produces a coordinated P wave, QRS complex, and T wave in regular succession.

This serves as the baseline for understanding all cardiac arrhythmias. A normal resting heart rate is 60–100 beats per minute with consistent intervals between each beat. Regular PQRST complexes indicate healthy, coordinated electrical conduction through the heart.
150–250 bpm

A re-entrant circuit within the AV node area uses fast and slow pathways to sustain a rapid heart rate

Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT)

A common condition mostly affecting younger, otherwise healthy people. An extra electrical connection causes the heart to beat rapidly at 150–250 times per minute.

Patients experience sudden onset of palpitations which may last minutes to hours. It can be cured with catheter ablation with success rates greater than 90%.
250–300 bpm flutter

A self-sustaining electrical circuit loops counter-clockwise around the right atrium at 250–300 beats per minute

Atrial Flutter

An electrical circuit revolves repeatedly around the right atrium at 250–300 beats per minute. The ventricles respond at a regular fraction of this rate, typically 2:1.

Especially common in older patients. Patients experience breathlessness, palpitations and fatigue. It can be cured with catheter ablation with success rates greater than 90%.
Irregular

Chaotic electrical impulses fire from the pulmonary veins and spread through the left atrium, causing an irregular heartbeat

Atrial Fibrillation (AF)

Chaotic electrical activation passes through the upper chambers, causing the atria to fibrillate rather than contract. This results in an irregular pulse and reduced pumping efficiency.

Symptoms include breathlessness and fatigue. In susceptible patients AF can increase the risk of stroke, which can be significantly reduced with blood thinning medication. Catheter ablation for AF is successful in reducing the burden of episodes significantly. Catheter ablation in paroxysmal AF the success is 70–85% and in persistent AF the success is 55–65%. Early treatment is important to secure the best outcomes.
150–250 bpm

Fast abnormal impulses originate from a focal point within the left ventricular muscle

Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)

A fast rhythm originating from the lower chambers of the heart. It produces wide, abnormal beats on the ECG and can cause palpitations, dizziness, breathlessness or blackouts.

In patients with underlying heart disease VT can be life-threatening and may require an implantable defibrillator (ICD). Catheter ablation can be highly effective in reducing VT episodes and ICD shocks, and is performed in specialist centres.
Occasional ectopics

An occasional premature beat fires from a focal point in the right ventricular outflow tract between normal beats

Ventricular Ectopics (PVCs)

Premature beats originating from the ventricles that interrupt an otherwise regular rhythm. Extremely common and usually benign in people with a structurally normal heart.

Patients often describe a missed beat, thud or flip in the chest. In some patients with very frequent ectopics, they can cause fatigue or breathlessness, and in rare cases may affect heart function. Treatment options include reassurance, medication, or catheter ablation which has excellent success rates.
Physiological pacing

A single lead passes through the right atrium to pace the His bundle and left bundle branch, activating the heart's own conduction system

Conduction System Physiological Pacing

His bundle or left bundle branch pacing delivers impulses directly to the heart's natural wiring system, producing a narrow, physiological QRS — preserving coordinated contraction of both ventricles.

Traditional pacemakers pace from the right ventricular apex, which can cause an unnatural contraction pattern over time. Conduction system pacing avoids this by activating the heart's own electrical pathways. Dr Kyriacou has specialist expertise in conduction system pacing.
Biventricular pacing

Three pacing leads coordinate the right atrium, right ventricle, and left ventricle via the coronary sinus to resynchronise the heart

Cardiac Resynchronisation Therapy (CRT)

CRT uses three pacing leads to coordinate the timing of both ventricles in patients with heart failure and electrical conduction delay, improving the heart's pumping efficiency.

By resynchronising ventricular contraction, CRT can significantly improve symptoms, exercise capacity, and quality of life. It is used alongside optimal medical therapy in selected patients with heart failure. Dr Kyriacou has specialist expertise in CRT implantation.
Paced

Cardiac Pacemakers

Blackouts and dizziness can result from problems with the heart's electrical system causing a very slow or paused heart rhythm. A pacemaker can restore a safe, reliable rhythm.

A small pulse generator is inserted under the skin, connected to leads placed into the heart through veins in the shoulder. The procedure is performed under local anaesthetic and prevents dangerous pauses in the heart rhythm.

Research Activity
